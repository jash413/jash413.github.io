ENDANGERED


Flat-headed cat
  • The flat-headed cat is a small wild cat that is found in the wetlands of Southeast Asia. They are threatened by habitat loss and degradation due to human activities, as well as hunting and trapping for their fur and body parts.
Fishing cat
  • The fishing cat is a medium-sized wild cat that is found in wetland habitats in South and Southeast Asia. They are threatened by habitat loss and degradation due to human activities, as well as poaching for their fur and body parts.
Iberian Lynx
  • The Iberian Lynx is one of the most endangered species of cats in the world, with an estimated population of only 150-250 adult individuals. It is found in southern Spain and Portugal and is considered as the most endangered species of cat in the world.

African golden cat
  • The African golden cat (Caracal aurata) is a medium-sized wild cat native to the rainforests of West and Central Africa. It is a solitary and elusive animal, and very little is known about its behavior in the wild.
    The African golden cat has a distinctive appearance, with short, reddish-brown fur that is marked with black spots and stripes. It has a long, bushy tail and large, rounded ears. It is about the size of a large domestic cat, typically weighing between 5 and 16 kg (11 to 35 lb).
    The African golden cat is a skilled hunter, feeding on a variety of prey including small mammals, birds, and reptiles. It is also known to hunt larger animals such as duikers and monkeys. The species is threatened by habitat loss and hunting, as well as by competition with other predators such as leopards and hyenas.

Snow leopard
  • The estimated population of this threatened species is somewhere between 2,710 and 3,386 individuals.1 The iconic snow leopard lives in the unbelievably cold habitats of alpine and subalpine areas of Central and South Asia, particularly the Tibetan Plateau and the Himalayas. Rarely ever seen in the wild based in part because of its elusive nature and also because so few remain, this animal's numbers continue to decrease despite conservation efforts.

Borneo Bay Cat
  • Only an estimated 2,200 mature endangered Borneo bay cats (Catopuma badia) currently live in their secluded range on the island of Borneo.5 These cats are the size of a large house cat and have a chestnut-colored body with a grayish-brown head. They have two dark stripes from the corners of their eyes to their whiskers. They also have a dark mark shaped like the letter M on the back of their head.
    Unfortunately, researchers know very little about bay cats, and few studies focus on the species. Indeed, the first photograph of a live Borneo bay cat was taken in 1998. Deforestation of its habitat for commercial logging and oil palm plantations constitutes the most significant threat to the species.
Marbled cat
  • The marbled cat, native to South and Southeast Asia, has been listed as vulnerable to extinction since 2002, and fewer than 10,000 mature individuals persist in the world.12 It is about the size of a house cat and lives in the branches of trees, where it hunts birds, squirrels, and reptiles. Many people compare the marbled cat to the clouded leopard because of similar markings, canine teeth, and habitats.
    Many marbled cats fall victim to snaring by humans who value the bones, meat, and fur. Thankfully, many countries prohibit hunting, which may help to slow its decline — but only if deforestation ceases as well. A loss of habitat proves an urgent threat to this arboreal species.
Tiger
  • Only an estimated 3,900 adult tigers remain in the wild despite being the most iconic cat species in the world, next to the African lion.6
    That number actually represents an increase attributed to ambitious conservation plans and better methods of survey. Poaching is the primary threat to tigers worldwide.7 In some areas, people believe that various tiger parts cure everything from epilepsy and insomnia to laziness and pimples. There's no evidence to support any medical use. Skins also bring in top dollar.

Andean mountain cat
  • Fewer than 1,400 endangered Andean cats remain.8 Before 1998, the only evidence scientists had that it existed at all were two photographs. These endangered cats only reach about 2 feet long and 18 pounds as adults.
    The appearance and high-altitude habitat call to mind the snow leopard. But unlike the snow leopard, there is far less conservation funding to help this cat. Two groups, the Andean Cat Alliance and the Small Cat Conservation Alliance, mainly assist with conservation efforts for this felid species. Habitat loss and degradation rank as the top reasons for the declining population.
Clouded leopard
  • The clouded leopard population is estimated to be less than 10,000 throughout Southeast Asia has been declared extinct in Taiwan.9 IUCN has listed the animal as vulnerable since 2008, and the main threats against it are habitat loss from extensive deforestation and commercial poaching for the wildlife trade. Populations of clouded leopard in Borneo have an advantage over ones in other areas due to the lack of tigers and other leopard species competing for resources.10
African lion
  • Not yet endangered but listed as vulnerable with only around 23,000 (at best) still living in the wild, lions face rapidly decreasing numbers.
    The king of the jungle has lost 30 to 50 percent of its population in the last two decades alone.
    Because of habitat loss and conflict with humans, most lions only inhabit eastern and southern Africa, with their numbers in severe decline. Conservation groups are working to preserve habitat so that lions have enough room to hunt and roam, but also to provide people with tools and knowledge for how to coexist with these big cats and reduce the number of deaths due to snaring.
